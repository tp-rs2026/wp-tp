Open Banking in New Zealand: A Guide for Businesses

Open Banking is changing how money moves in New Zealand. For businesses, it means a new payment option that bypasses card networks entirely — reducing fees, simplifying settlement, and giving customers more choice at the point of sale. Whether you run a café, a retail store, or a service business, here’s what you need to know.

What is Open Banking?

Open Banking lets regulated third parties access bank account data and initiate payments through secure APIs, with the account holder’s explicit consent. Rather than building proprietary connections to each bank, third-party providers use standardised interfaces that banks are required to support.

In New Zealand, Open Banking is managed by Payments NZ through the API Centre. The API Centre sets the technical standards, security requirements, and consent frameworks that govern how third parties connect to banks. The major participating banks include ANZ, ASB, BNZ, Kiwibank, and Westpac — which between them serve the vast majority of New Zealand’s personal and business bank accounts.

How does a bank-to-bank payment work?

A bank-to-bank payment through Open Banking is straightforward. Here’s the typical flow from the customer’s perspective:

  1. The customer chooses to pay via their bank at checkout.
  2. They’re redirected to their own bank’s app or website.
  3. They authenticate using their bank’s security — Face ID, fingerprint, or password.
  4. They review the payment details and confirm.
  5. The money moves directly from their bank account to the merchant’s bank account.

There’s no card number involved, no card network processing the transaction, and no terminal required. The payment travels on banking rails, not card rails.

What does this mean for fees?

To understand why Open Banking matters for merchants, it helps to see how a typical card payment flows. When a customer taps their Visa or Mastercard, the payment passes through multiple intermediaries: the card network (Visa or Mastercard), the issuing bank (the customer’s bank), and the acquiring bank or payment processor (the merchant’s side). Each layer takes a cut — interchange fees, scheme fees, and acquirer margins. By the time the merchant receives the funds, 2–3% of the transaction value has been absorbed by these fees.

An Open Banking payment removes most of these layers. The money moves from the consumer’s bank directly to the merchant’s bank. There’s no interchange fee, no card scheme fee, and no acquirer margin. The result is a payment that costs a fraction of what card processing charges — a meaningful difference for businesses operating on tight margins.

Is it safe?

Yes. Open Banking payments are built on strong authentication. Every payment requires the customer to authenticate directly with their own bank using the bank’s own security — biometrics, PIN, or password. The third-party provider that initiates the payment never sees the customer’s banking credentials and never has access to their account.

No card details are shared or stored at any point in the process. There’s no card number to be stolen, no CVV to be compromised. The customer approves each payment individually through their trusted banking app. The entire framework is regulated under New Zealand’s banking and financial services standards, and the API Centre’s own security requirements add an additional layer of governance.

Who’s using it?

Open Banking payments are live in New Zealand today. Companies like Akahu and BlinkPay provide the API infrastructure that connects third-party providers to the banks. Businesses can accept bank payments through platforms that integrate with these providers, without needing to build bank connections themselves.

Adoption is growing steadily. As more consumers become familiar with paying from their bank account — rather than reaching for a card — businesses that offer the option are seeing real take-up, particularly for higher-value transactions where card fees bite hardest.

What about card payments?

Open Banking doesn’t replace cards — it adds an alternative. Some customers will prefer bank payments, especially when they see it helps the businesses they frequent. Others will continue to prefer cards out of habit, for the credit facility, or to earn rewards points. Both are valid choices.

For businesses, the smartest approach is to offer both. Accepting bank payments gives you access to lower-cost transactions, while keeping cards ensures you don’t turn away customers who prefer them. The more payment options you support, the more flexibility your customers have — and the more control you have over your payment costs.

What tapara does

Tapara is a member of the API Centre and uses Open Banking as its bank payment rail. Merchants on the tapara platform can accept bank-to-bank payments at near-zero fees — no terminal required, no card network in the middle. Customers pay by scanning a QR code or checking in through the tapara app, then confirming the payment through their own bank.

Card payments are also supported via Tap to Pay on iPhone, so merchants have a complete payment solution in a single app. Whether a customer wants to pay by bank or by card, tapara handles both.
